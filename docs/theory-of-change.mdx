# Theory Of Change

The goal of every social [intervention](intervention.mdx) is to improve some developmental outcome(s). A **theory of change** is the causal path from inputs to outcomes.

For instance, let's assume your intervention is to give farmers access to better and timely advice so that farm yields improve. The theory of change from advice to yield improvement could be defined as:

1. Context-aware advice will be made available for farmers

2. Farmers will access the advice

3. The advice will influence farmer behaviour

4. Farm yields will improve

Alternatively, you may be looking to improve an existing program with an additional intervention.

For instance, lets assume you have health workers tasked with visiting pregnant women to encourage them to make regular visits to a health facility so that mother and child health outcomes improve. However, your health workers are not visiting all pregnant women. Your intervention is to have their supervisors push them to complete their visits. The theory of change could be defined as:

1. A pregnancy is registered by a health worker

2. A missed visit is flagged

3. The supervisor reminds the worker to make the visit

4. The worker makes the visit

5. The pregnant woman visits the health facility

In this case, the intervention begins at step 2, assuming step 1 is happening well.

:::tip What is the theory of change for your intervention?

Describe the the chain of events from inputs to outcomes. The chain can be longer than four steps!

:::

## Beneficiary vs User vs Actor

Every social intervention has a target **beneficiary**, but the beneficiary may not be the primary **user** of your product. In some cases, there may even be other **actors** in the chain.

In the farmer advice example above, the farmer is both the user and the beneficiary. But, for instance, what if agriculture extension workers were to access the advice and communicate it to farmers? That makes the extension workers the *user,* while the farmer remains the *beneficiary*. Hence, the product interface has to be tuned to the extension worker's user persona, while the advice has to be tailored for farmers.

In the healthcare example above, the pregnant woman is the *beneficiary*, the supervisor is the *user*, and the health worker is an *actor*. Hence, the product interface has to be tuned for supervisors.

:::tip Who is your user?

Use the chain of events to list all the actors involved in your intervention, and identify the primary user of the product.

:::

## Metrics

A social intervention is more likely to be successful if progress is measured at each step/level because it will show what is going well, and what isn't.

Capturing objective **metrics** at each level will enable you to track the effect of iterative improvements, as well as the effect of changes at one level on the metrics of another level.

For instance, the farmer advice itervention could be measured at each step as such:

|   | Level                                                   | Metrics                                                  |
| - | ------------------------------------------------------- | -------------------------------------------------------- |
| 1 | Context-aware advice will be made available for farmers | <p>Advice accuracy</p><p>Relevance</p>                   |
| 2 | Farmers will access the advice                          | <p>Questions asked</p><p>Conversation length</p>         |
| 3 | The advice will influence farmer behaviour              | <p>Practices improved</p><p>Change in farming inputs</p> |
| 4 | Farm yields will improve                                | Crop cut                                                 |

:::tip How would you measure progress at each step for your intervention?

List metrics that you can objectively measure and track over time. The higher-level metrics should align with your business/organization goals so progress will be meaningful.

:::
