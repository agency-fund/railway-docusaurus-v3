# User Research

Product teams rely on analytics to tell them *what* users do, but not *why*. User research fills that gap — interviews reveal what users say they'll do, while analytics captures what they actually do. Used together, they give you a complete picture.

The biggest mistake teams make is treating research as a post-launch activity. Bringing research in early prevents costly rework: discovering that none of the options your team built will work for users is expensive. Done continuously, user research helps you prioritise the right features, design more intuitive flows, and build products users actually return to.

## How to do it

### 1. Define Your Research Questions

Before recruiting anyone, write down what you need to know. Questions should be specific and tied to a real product decision. "Why are users dropping off during onboarding?" is useful. "What do users think about the app?" is not.

Common triggers:
- Drop-off or low engagement in your analytics funnel
- A feature you're considering building with significant engineering cost
- Confusing patterns in support tickets or NPS feedback
- A new user segment you don't yet understand

### 2. Choose Your Method

Use interviews to understand problems and solutions; use analytics to prioritise and measure.

| Method | Best for |
|---|---|
| User interviews | Motivations, mental models, and barriers |
| User journey mapping | Understanding the full experience across all touchpoints — before, during, and after using your product. Unlike funnels (which track conversion metrics), journey maps capture the emotions, needs, and context *around* each step. |
| Usability testing | Friction in specific flows or features |
| Contextual observation | How users behave in their real environment |
| Surveys | Validating patterns across a larger sample |

For most social impact products, **interviews and contextual observation** are the most valuable starting point — especially when users are in low-connectivity or low-literacy environments where digital surveys are impractical.

### 3. Recruit the Right Users

Don't only speak with your most active users. Recruit across the full spectrum: power users, occasional users, and people who dropped off or never activated.

Use existing data — usage logs, admin records, support tickets — to identify and segment recruits. If you lack data, a short phone survey can act as a screening tool.

### 4. Conduct Research

Run 45–60 minute sessions with a semi-structured guide: a handful of open-ended questions with probes, not a rigid script. Let users define what matters before steering toward your specific questions.

In each session, listen for:
- **Goals** — what the user is trying to achieve
- **Constraints** — what makes it hard
- **Workarounds** — how they cope with gaps in your product

### 5. Build Personas and Share

After 5–8 sessions, run a synthesis session with your team to cluster observations into themes and define 3–5 user personas. A persona should capture:

- **Who they are** — role, context, level of digital confidence
- **Their goal** — what they're trying to achieve with your product
- **Their barriers** — what gets in the way
- **A representative quote** — something they actually said that captures their mindset

Well-built personas serve three purposes: they re-orient the team away from day-to-day feature work, create alignment across product, engineering, and design on what users actually need, and ensure research gets used in decisions rather than filed away. When the whole team shares a common understanding of who they're building for, they can make better decisions faster without needing sign-off at every turn. Base personas on patterns across interviews, not on any single person.

The final output should be a short deck that links each persona and theme to a concrete design implication. Research only creates value when it changes what the team builds.

- [Typeform](https://typeform.com/) — screener surveys and lightweight quantitative validation
