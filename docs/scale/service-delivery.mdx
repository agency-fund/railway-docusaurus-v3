# Service Delivery

## What we are learning

Many lighthouses begin as the sole operator of their own program, delivering it directly, with their own staff, to their own users. The harder transition comes when they hand pieces of that delivery to a government or another operator while trying to protect the fidelity that made the program work in the first place. Service delivery is the work of deciding what belongs in the software platform, what belongs in the workflows a third party has to adopt, and what still has to be delivered by people on your team.

We are learning that it helps to break a program into three layers.

1.  **The platform** is the structured scaffold, and it seems to be the best way to reinforce fidelity and protect the probability of impact once someone other than you is running the program.

2.  **The workflows** are the guardrails: the rules frontline workers, supervisors, and administrators follow so they can deliver the way you have.

3.  **The services** are the things you still do by hand, the training, the quality assurance, the support that keeps the model intact once it leaves your control.

The service layer tends to fall into a few categories. These include, onboarding and setup, training and certification, quality assurance, and the more complex support that cannot be standardized. Naming these early is what lets an organization see what it will still have to provide even once the software is in a partner's hands.

We are also learning that services are not a one-time cost. There is usually a larger upfront cost for onboarding, setup, and localization, followed by recurring costs for the things that continue, such as quality audits, reports, dashboards, refresher training. Both seem worth naming early, because the platform and the services are priced separately and often a government budgets for them in different ways.

This connects directly to going to market. It is not fruitful to invest resources in selling a solution an organization cannot deliver. Especially since at scale a delivery failure usually costs more than an unclosed deal. It is part of why go-to-market work tends to activate only once a program can actually be delivered.

We are also learning there’s a lot contingent on who the customer actually is. The person who uses the product is often not the person who pays for it. Inside a government, the operator, the programmatic lead, and the budget holder can be three different people, sometimes sitting in different ministries. An advantage framed for someone who understands the program area lands differently with a finance official looking only at the budget. What appears to help is defining an ideal customer profile, who they are, what they are accountable for, what they can already do, so that the platform and services are designed for the operator who will actually hold the program.

## What we are still testing

We are testing how a lighthouse defines its minimum viable stack, the smallest set of platform features and services that still carries its core advantages, and how much of the service layer can move into the platform over time so the model becomes more standardized and self-serve.

We are testing where experimentation should sit once a government takes over. Some organizations expect to keep testing close to themselves at first and to open it up only when a partner is mature enough to run it well. Whether that capability transfers, and when, is still an open question.

We are testing how the arrangement changes as delivery expands. What works for a single district run by one kind of partner may not hold when the program moves to a new geography, serves a far larger population, or is run by a team with different capabilities, and the platform, the services, and the commercial terms may all have to shift with it.
