# Sales

## What we are learning

Sales is the work of converting a scaling opportunity, or a promising government relationship, into a signed and funded contract.

We are learning that public-sector sales is not a linear journey from identifying an opportunity to securing a contract. The decisive work often happens earlier and less visibly than a conventional pipeline suggests, before a formal opportunity is published and while the problem, delivery model, financing, terms of reference, and evaluation criteria are still being shaped. This means lighthouses may need to remain engaged over a long and uncertain period, as procurement timelines and priorities evolve. This includes working with decision-makers before procurement begins, helping define the problem and shape an approach that supports effective delivery. By the time a request for proposals is public, many of the most important decisions may already have been made.

For many lighthouses, the constraint is therefore not that their sales capability needs to be sharpened. It is that the function does not yet exist.

Without a dedicated sales function, lighthouses may struggle to invest in the work that precedes a formal opportunity. Such as building relationships with government counterparts, understanding who controls the budget, and remaining present while the terms are still being written. It is also imperative to spend time in person with the right stakeholders to scope these relationships.

Governments are keen towards products that can demonstrate credible in-country delivery, as opposed to buying global solutions. A strong product and evidence base may not be enough without a clear local delivery pathway. In-person engagement is particularly important, as face-to-face sessions can help lighthouses build trust and demonstrate localisation efforts.

We’re also learning how critical stakeholder mapping is to sales. A helpful champion is not always the buyer. Lighthouses often invest heavily in a funder-side counterpart who supports the idea, opens doors, and carries it into government, yet holds no authority to award the work. Contracting authority often sits within multiple government departments. Distinguishing the actors who influence, design, and approve an opportunity from those who ultimately sign it is one of the harder parts of the job.

Our early learnings suggest that lighthouses may fare better when they treat each pilot as the basis for the next negotiation and begin planning for government ownership and financing from the outset, rather than addressing these questions later.

## What we are still testing

Most opportunities we have seen begin with a champion, often a funder, introducing a lighthouse to a government counterpart. We do not yet know how reliably those introductions convert into signed contracts, or how often they stall at a warm first meeting.

Government sales seem to depend on sustained local engagement, and a relevant solution and a good introduction may not, on their own, be enough.

We are also testing how sales differs across government and private-sector buyers. A commercial partner, such as a telecom or private company, may respond to a case built around customer acquisition, revenue, or strategic advantage. A corporate social responsibility team may respond to a case built around mission, reach, and impact.
