# Marketing

## What we are learning

In the private and technology sectors, marketing is how an organization communicates the value of its offer to its intended buyers.

In contrast, one of our first observations when examining how nonprofits market themselves was the language and framing they use to demonstrate value, for instance “cost-effective impact” and “10x social return on investment.” These frames are designed to appeal to impact-oriented philanthropic funders, instead of government buyers.

Meeting government demand means designing and communicating solutions around the problems a government is actually trying to solve. Borrowing from private-sector practice, this means articulating the value of the intervention in the buyer's own terms, for example, showing how a dashboard helps officials track delivery.

Our analysis suggests that governments look for different forms of value:

- **Operability.** Whether the solution is something they can actually run, a dashboard they can see their own data in, a system that fits what they already use

- **Cost against the status quo.** Not impact per dollar, but what this costs relative to what they already spend to address the same problem.

- **Coverage and reach.** Progress on the metrics they are accountable for, how many people served, how much of the population reached

- **Political viability.** Whether the solution helps them deliver on a visible mandate or commitment

- **In country evidence:** Whether local results generate confidence that it can work within existing systems and constraints.

Organizations often reach first for effect sizes because these are the forms of evidence they have spent years producing and defending. Yet government buyers may be asking a different set of questions. A procurement officer, for example, is typically accountable for whether the money was spent within budget, if the service reached the people it was meant to, or if it can be delivered on time without the project stalling.

Beyond this, governments’ appetite for risk differs fundamentally from that of philanthropic funders. The downside of failure often carries far greater weight than the potential “upside” of success.

## What we are still testing

We are also learning that lighthouses do not identify every opportunity themselves. Some opportunities emerge through existing funder and partner networks. Our hypothesis is that lighthouses therefore need to be sufficiently visible and credible within the right networks to be considered when these opportunities arise.

We are also exploring how this extends beyond government altogether, to World Bank Task Team Leaders and private-sector Corporate Social Responsibility partners. Reputation is also a key factor for governments, and credibility can be borrowed from a well-regarded partner. Precision Development, for example, was brought in through a World Bank loan and drew on the Bank's credibility.

In-person sessions also appear to be critical marketing efforts, as they create opportunities to build trust, and develop human connections.
