# Discovery

## What we are learning

Many nonprofits articulate their “path to scale” in terms of working with the government, but finding the right entry points can be challenging, especially when governments are cash-strapped.

We have found that even today, resource mobilization within public systems are an order of magnitude larger than philanthropy. Yet despite the size of the opportunity, many evidence-based organizations remain reliant on grants.

Discovery is the work of identifying government and multilateral opportunities worth pursuing before a lighthouse invests time and resources in them.

What we have learned is that the constraint is rarely a shortage of opportunities. Across government priorities and financing pipelines, there are often many possible entry points, particularly where the need for cost-effective solutions is greatest.

The harder question is which few opportunities are real, well-timed, and aligned with what the lighthouse is positioned to win.

This pattern suggests that relationships may not be an advantage on their own. Their value may depend significantly on whether they are activated at a point in the financing process when program design and delivery terms are still open to influence.

A policy window becomes viable when several conditions align.

- Accessibility of resources

- Political demand

- Technical feasibility of delivery (e.g., does context have ability to implement your intervention, such as access to web services)

**Timing must be navigated**

*Timing inside a financing process may matter as much as the relationship behind it.*

![Policy window timing diagram](/img/scale-discovery-policy-window.png)

Entering during the design phase creates stronger prospects for engagement, while eligibility criteria, delivery models, and implementation requirements are still open to influence. Once a procurement window is live, the program’s terms may already be fixed, making it too late to shape the window meaningfully. Project timelines routinely go off track too.

## What we are still testing

We have thus far focused on development finance institutions. Our hypothesis that there is demand for social innovation within the World Bank appears to be supported. We are now in the early stages of testing whether governments themselves demand services that can help them improve public service delivery through procurement reform.
