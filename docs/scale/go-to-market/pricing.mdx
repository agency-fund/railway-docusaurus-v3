# Pricing

## What we are learning

Many nonprofits have built their services through philanthropic funding, and have had fewer opportunities to determine what those services should cost when a government or institutional buyer is the one paying.

Pricing is the work of setting a number a government can budget for, grounded in what the service costs to deliver but justified by the value the buyer receives, measured against the status quo.

We began by assuming pricing was primarily a costing exercise, and that a lighthouse with a clear grasp of its unit economics could arrive at a defensible price. We are learning that unit economics establishes an important cost floor, but does not set the price on its own. The price also depends on how the lighthouse comprehends and communicates the value of its product. That value is anchored to a specific comparator: what the government currently spends to achieve the same outcome, or what it spends despite the outcome remaining unmet.

We are also learning that philanthropic subsidies can set an unintended precedent. When programs are delivered at no cost to the government, a lighthouse organization may inadvertently establish the expectation that the service is free.

Reversing that expectation can be harder than introducing a price from the outset, because the organization is arguing against a precedent it helped create.

Lighthouses therefore need to distinguish clearly between:

- The full commercial price of the service

- The contribution funded by government or another buyer

- The costs covered through a time-bound pilot subsidy

- The additional costs of localization, implementation, infrastructure, or technical support

- Add on premium features

In this early view, sustainable pricing means making each cost visible, setting a price above the cost floor, and linking it to a value the buyer recognizes. A margin may then support the innovation needed to improve the service quality over time.

## What we are still testing

We are determining whether a menu of clearly defined options is more effective than presenting buyers with a single price. In practice, this could mean offering packages with named deliverables, stated prices, and clear boundaries.

This approach is testing whether a lighthouse can hold a value-based price in front of a government at all, or whether procurement pressure tends to pull the number back toward cost.

Some government processes reward the lowest compliant bid regardless of the value on offer, and we do not yet know how often the buyer who weighs cost against outcome is the one actually across the table.
