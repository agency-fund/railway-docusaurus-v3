# Getting Started

Improving a product or program is a continuous cycle: understand your users, build and test solutions, measure whether they work, and bring in the right people to sustain progress. This section covers each part of that cycle.

## The Cycle

### 1. Build your team → [Team and Hiring](./team-and-hiring)

The right team is a prerequisite for everything else. Before you can do good user research or run rigorous experiments, you need people who own those functions.

**Start here if:** you're scaling a product team, defining roles, or thinking about what skills to hire for next.

### 2. Understand your users → [User Research](./user-research)

Analytics tells you *what* users do — user research tells you *why*. Interviews, usability testing, and contextual observation reveal the motivations, barriers, and mental models that drive user behaviour.

**Start here if:** you're designing something new, seeing unexpected drop-off, or making decisions based on assumptions rather than evidence.

### 3. Build and prototype solutions → [Coding and Prototyping](./coding-and-prototyping)

Before committing to a full build, prototype and validate. Lightweight tools let you test ideas quickly with real users before investing significant engineering time.

**Start here if:** you have a validated problem and need to move from research insight to something users can interact with.

### 4. Manage your product → [Product Management](./product-management)

Good product management connects user needs, business goals, and engineering capacity into a coherent roadmap. It keeps the team aligned on what to build next and why.

**Start here if:** you need to structure your backlog, improve prioritisation, or establish a clearer product development process.

### 5. Test your changes → [Experimentation](./experimentation)

When you make changes, you need to know whether they worked. A/B testing and other experimentation methods let you confirm that improvements are real — not noise — before rolling them out to everyone.

**Start here if:** you're iterating on an existing product and want to be confident your changes are having the intended effect.

### 6. Apply behavioural insights → [Behavioral Science](./behavioral-science)

Behavioural science provides a toolkit for understanding why users make the choices they do and how to design for better outcomes — from reducing friction to building habits.

**Start here if:** you've done the basics well but want to move beyond usability and into what actually changes behaviour.

---

## Next Steps

If you're starting from scratch, work through these in order:

1. Define who owns product and research on your team
2. Run your first round of user research to understand who you're building for
3. Prototype and test a solution before committing to a full build
4. Instrument your product and assign ownership of key metrics
5. Design your first experiment when you're ready to validate a change
